# GENS-2101 Master Study Guide: Natural Resources Management

## Core Foundations

### 1. Key Definitions & Frameworks

* **Nature vs. Environment**:
  * *Nature*: Totality of phenomena not created by humans.
  * *Environment*: Surrounding conditions (natural, built, social) affecting organisms.
* **NRM (Derek Armitage)**: "An adaptive and collaborative process through which actors across scales manage social-ecological systems to sustain ecosystem functions, livelihoods, and resilience in the face of change."
* **Socio-Ecological Systems (SES)**: A framework recognizing that humans are *part of* nature, not apart from it. Emphasizes interdependence, non-linearity, and feedbacks.
* **The Cornucopian Fallacy**: The belief that resources are infinite and the Earth is a limitless sink for waste.

### 2. Governance vs. Management

* **Governance**: "Decides." Sets rules, goals, and authority. Handles legitimacy and stakeholder mapping.
* **Management**: "Delivers." Implementation, technical tools, monitoring, and enforcement.
* **Relationship**: Governance shapes management; Management feeds back into governance (monitoring results, revealing conflicts).

### 3. The Laws of Thermodynamics in NRM

* **1st Law (Conservation)**: "Nothing disappears." Waste, pollution, and emissions are unavoidable.
* **2nd Law (Entropy)**: "Everything degrades." Energy transformations lead to a decrease in usable energy.
* **Application**: Management must focus on slowing down irreversible degradation (Sustainability) and conserving high-quality energy/materials.

## Power, Actors, and Scale

### 4. Stakeholder Mapping & Power

* **Three Forms of Power (Robert Cox)**:
    1. **Ideational**: Ideas, norms, and narratives (e.g., "Sustainable Development").
    2. **Institutional**: Formal rules, laws, and organizations (e.g., the State).
    3. **Material**: Money, weapons, technology, extracted resources.
* **Dominant Actor Triangle**: States (.gov), Private Sector (.com), Civil Society (.org). Balance between these is required for sustainable outcomes.

### 5. Multi-Level Governance in Canada

* **Local**: Implementation, stewardship, site-specific knowledge (TEK).
* **Provincial**: **Primary Authority** over natural resources in Canada (Licensing, harvest limits).
* **National**: Federal law (Fisheries Act, SARA), Indigenous rights (Section 35), national science.
* **Transboundary**: International Joint Commission (IJC), trade agreements (Softwood Lumber).
* **International**: Global norms (UN-REDD), certification (FSC), climate agreements (Paris Agreement).

## Resource Dynamics

### 6. Ecosystem Management vs. Traditional Management

* **Traditional (Command and Control)**:
  * Linear "take-make-waste" model.
  * Maximizes single-commodity yield (e.g., Timber Primacy).
  * Centralized, expert-driven decision making.
* **Ecosystem Approach**:
  * Holistic, landscape-level view.
  * Recognizes ecosystems as dynamic (not a static "climax community").
  * Maintains integrity and multiple services (goods + services).

### 7. The Anthropocene & Entropy Export

* **Anthropocene**: A new geological epoch where human activity is the dominant force shaping Earth systems.
* **Extroversion**: Outside actors (Biosphere People) encroaching on local resources (Ecosystem People).
* **Entropy Export**: Developed economies maintain order by exporting ecological disorder (pollution, deforestation) to the Global South or future generations.
* **Palm Oil Case Study**: Represents "metabolic acceleration"—linear, fast flows that overwhelm natural sinks.

## Best Practices & Learning

### 8. Good Governance Benchmarks (UN)

* Participation, Transparency, Equity, Rule of Law, Accountability, Coherency, Responsiveness, Integration, Ethical Considerations.

### 9. Learning Loops

* **Single-loop**: Are we doing the *thing* right? (Efficiency).
* **Double-loop**: Are we doing the *right thing*? (Goal adjustment).
* **Triple-loop**: How do we *know* what is right? (Paradigm shift/Reflexivity).

### 10. Key Takeaways

* "Humans usually attempt to manage the *interaction* between humans and the environment." (Mitchell)
* "Everything is political."
* "No single level can achieve sustainability alone."
