# Introduction to Natural Resource Management for Atlantic Salmon

## 1. Ecological Significance & Bioindicators

Atlantic salmon (*Salmo salar*) are a keystone "bioindicator" species. Their health is a direct reflection of ecosystem purity, requiring cold, clean water. A decline in salmon populations signals broader environmental degradation (pollution, temperature rise). They are the "canary in the coal mine" for both freshwater rivers and marine environments.

## 2. Sustainable Resource Management

Effective management requires a holistic "Ridge-to-Reef" approach, protecting the entire life cycle from spawning streams to ocean feeding grounds. Key strategies include:

- **Habitat Restoration**: Removing dams and restoring river connectivity.
- **Pollution Control**: Minimizing agricultural runoff and industrial effluent.
- **Genetic Integrity**: Protecting wild stocks from interbreeding with farm escapees.

## 3. Sustainable Exports & Quality Control

As a high-value export (over $27B globally), maintaining quality is critical for market dominance.

- **Quality Control (QC)**: Involves rigorous monitoring of flesh color (pigmentation), texture, and contaminant levels.
- **Sustainability Certifications**: Adherence to **ASC (Aquaculture Stewardship Council)** standards ensures minimal environmental impact.
- **Innovations**: Land-based Recirculating Aquaculture Systems (RAS) are emerging to produce export-grade salmon with zero marine impact.
